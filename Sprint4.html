Problem Solving in the Field

A Wild Blockage Appears

Problem: I had to write some code that would go through a list of numbers and return either Fizz, Bang or FizzBang depending on if the number was divisible by 5 or 3. While I had a fairly clear understanding of the logic and steps involved in solving this, I stumbled on the numbers that were divisible by both 3 and 5. Eventually I discovered that I had my step order the wrong way around and managed to solve the problem with a quick cut and paste!

Techniques Used: Rubber Ducky, Pseudocode, Trying something, trying something again, and again. Googling and finally good old console.logging to keep track of what was going on.

During the Process: Initially I was quite confident that I could easily solve the problem and when I ran up against issues I searched for wrong problems. This lead to a bit of frustration and I got fairly bogged down. Luckily I trusted my gut at one stage and tried the order swap and it worked!

Lessons Learnt: Mistakes can crop up in various stages of the process, and even if I feel fairly confident in how it all comes ogether I should still check out all avenues instead of leaving blind spots because I think what I did there was correct.

Take the Wins

Problem: I was asked to write code to automate a gradebook for students. While I do not think myself qualified in even the aguest sense to be calling my work elegant, I managed to get a good grasp of what was required and what steps to follow to achieve my goals. My pseudocode worked well and I was able to finish up the task without too many issues - which was a very exciting and hugely encouraging.

Techniques Used: Rubber Ducky, PseudoCode, Trying Something and Google.

During the Process: During the process I felt quite calm, not sure of myself at all, but certain enough that the answer was just around the corner to get into the focus zone and put it all together efficiently.

Lessons Learnt: Not a technical or problem solving skill, but rather than I can do this and that coding can be as much fun as I had hoped it would be!

Problem Solving Techniques

PseudoCode: I enjoy using pseudocode and have tried to make it the second step in all the problems I have to solve. That being said there is a lot of room for improvement in the way I plan and lay the pseudocode out.

Try Something: I do this a lot, but I don’t find it as effective at this stage. Almost as if it is a bit of a dice roll considering my low level of coding skills, but sometimes that quick attempt yields some pretty pleasing results!

Rubber Ducky: My go-to first step, I feel that the process of pulling apart a problem during the story telling phase of RubberDucky gives a good understanding of what needs to be done before the brain kicks in with recons and plans. This understanding works really well with pseudocode as I can use them together to stop myself from going down rabbit holes which might not even yield the results I was hoping for.

Error Messages: I feel I make good use of error messages. Or at least I try to, as they can be pretty inscrutable at times still. I am however very confident that my skills in reading them and using the information to quickly hone in on an issue will only grow experience.

Console.log: Another technique I use all the time. Not entirely sure if it is good practice to do so but I am constantly checking the results of functions or logging out alerts when my code reaches certain spots. To me it feels like a very valuable window into what my code is doing, or let’s be honest - what it ISN’T doing.

Google: Where would I be without googling - when I started this course I was blown away by how granular you can get with your search terms and still get solid results. There is truth to the statement that every problem you have, someone else already had and probably solved. The hardest part would be sifting through them all to find the applicable solutions - but this is also a skill I expect will develop as I gain experience.

Peer Assistance: A bit of a gap in my arsenal here as I have not had to do this yet. There is just so much help available online that I have not had to resort to asking my peers for help. I suspect that this will change once we hit bootcamp and are all together.

Coach Assistance: I have not had to do this yet either - but feel that I would be pretty confident in asking assistance or clarification if I need to do so.

Improving through Reflection: This is a pretty vital step to learning and almost takes place despite what I’m doing. I often find myself thinking of different ways of tackling a coding task while cooking or just running through a problem in my head while driving. I feel that this mulling over helps me incorporate new skills into my matrix of meaning.

Neuroplasticity & The Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Understanding the principles of neuroplasticity brings two main benefits. The first benefit aligns extremely closely with that of the Growth Mindset, in that it opens that mental door inside a person that leads to development through all stages of life. The second, and more practical benefit it that by understanding how neuroplasticity works, is that once a person becomes familiar with how it takes place and what kind of actions and behaviours encourage neuroplasticity, they can actively seek out, and embrace such situations and behaviours to harness this incredible ability for their own continued improvement.

Principles of neuroplasticity which may be extremely useful in a learning environment is knowing that there are two distinct stages of learning (short term and long term) and that each of these stages have techniques you can use improve performance. Short term learning can be encouraged through the use of novelty and by applying short bursts of fully focussed engagement. The second phase of neuroplastic development involves the physical growth and development of brain structures and as such thrives on repeated practice over a time, and from the continued integration of new knowledge into a cohesive contextual model.

The following methods are some of the ways by which you can increase the effectiveness of neuroplasticity: Intermittent Fasting offers a range of cognitive benefits such as the promotion of neuron growth and synaptic adaptation. Learning a new skill, such as juggling or playing an instrument triggers activity across a number of brain areas and in so doing enhances their interconnectivity which in turn can lead to the formation of new neural networks. Non-dominant hand exercises encourages the growth of entirely new neural pathways, and strengthens the connections between neurons. Sleeping well aids in the retention of what you have learnt as it encourages the growth of new connections between neurons, and thereby increasing the transfer of information.

Source: What is Neuroplasticity? A Psychologist Explains.

The Growth Mindset

Growth mindset is the faith you have in your own ability to grow and develop. This is hugely important as it forms he motivational backbone for virtually all human development. Having a growth mindset is also extremely relevant in our current situation as there would be no purpose in even trying to learn something new if you didn’t believe that you had the ability to do so.

This belief isn’t a simple on/off switch though and by integrating the principles of a growth mindset in a mindful way you are able to find areas where you might be holding yourself back due to a lack of self confidence, or to give you that final morale boost to keep cracking away at problem which seems unsolvable.

It was this last realisation that I found most surprising during my research and I will be focusing a lot of effort during my learning journey towards finding such self limiting beliefs in myself and swapping them out for more complete and resilient growth mindset versions.
